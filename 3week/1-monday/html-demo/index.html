Resistors

Resistors are passive electrical components used to limit electrical current in an electronic circuit. They implement electrical resistance as a circuit element. Resistors are typically used to reduce current flow, adjust signal levels, divide voltages, or terminate transmission lines among other uses. Their resistance values are measured in units called ohms (Î©). The value or resistance level of a resistor is often identified by a color code that is typically printed on its surface. The larger the resistance value a resistor has, the more it opposes (i.e., resists) the current flow. There are different types of resistors based on the material they are made from, their power rating, and their tolerance (the acceptable deviation from the stamped value). Some common types include fixed resistors, variable resistors, carbon composition resistors, metal film resistors, wire-wound resistors, and semiconductor resistors.

Capacitors

Capacitors are passive electronic components that store electrical energy in an electric field. The process of storing energy in the capacitor is known as "charging", and involves electric charges of equal magnitude, but opposite sign, building up on each plate. Capacitors are commonly used in electronic circuits for blocking direct current while allowing alternating current to pass, in filter networks, for energy storage, tuners, and in power conditioning, among other applications. They are available in various forms, sizes, and materials for different applications. Capacitors consist of two conductive plates separated by an insulating material called a dielectric. The conductive plates can be thin films of metal, alloy foil, or aluminum foil, while dielectrics can be made of various materials such as air, paper, plastic, glass, ceramic, or even a vacuum. The effectiveness of a capacitor is known as its capacitance, which is measured in farads (F), named after English scientist Michael Faraday. The capacitance value essentially tells you how much energy the capacitor can store.

Inductors

Inductors are electrical components that store energy in a magnetic field when an electric current flows through them. They are often used in electrical circuits due to their property of resisting changes in the current. Structurally, inductors are typically made of a coil of conducting material, like copper wire, usually wound around a core made from iron, air, or other materials.

Diodes

Diodes are semiconductor devices that allow current to flow in one direction but not the other. They are used in several electronic circuits and devices and have two terminals, anode (positive) and cathode (negative). The fundamental property of a diode is its tendency to conduct electric current in only one direction. Functionally, diodes work by having an "on" state, where they allow current to flow, and an "off" state, where they prevent it. When voltage is applied to the diode in one direction, it becomes forward-biased and allows current to flow. When voltage is applied in the reverse direction, the diode becomes reverse-biased, blocking current. Diodes have a multitude of applications including power conversion (rectification), voltage regulation, switching, signal modulation, and many others.

Transistors

Transistors are semiconductor devices used to amplify or switch electronic signals and electrical power. They are one of the basic building blocks of modern electronics. It is composed of semiconductor material usually with at least three terminals for connection to an external circuit. A voltage or current applied to one pair of the transistor's terminals controls the current through another pair of terminals. Transistors are categorized into two types: Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) and Field-Effect Transistor (FET). The key functions they perform include signal amplification, regulation of electrical signals, and acting as a switch.
